Tracking read-time and article→article conversion
You can't get dwell time or read-depth from your server — it requires a lightweight client-side beacon embedded in published content. Three things to capture:
Engaged time, not wall-clock. Naive "time on page" overcounts every tab someone left open. Track active time via heartbeats that pause when the tab is hidden (Page Visibility API) or the user is idle. That's the number that actually means "they read it."
Scroll depth. Fire milestones at 25/50/75/100% scrolled. Combined with engaged time, this gives you a read-through estimate — did they consume it or bounce after the first paragraph. If you instrument the <h2> positions, you even get which section they dropped off at, which ties directly back to the review service's section-level defects (now you know thin sections not by the judge's guess but by where real readers quit).
The mechanics: a small JS script firing page_view, heartbeat, scroll_milestone, read_complete events to an ingestion endpoint, batched, using navigator.sendBeacon so the final event survives page unload. These map onto the recordEngagement fields you already have (timeOnPage, bounceRate) plus a couple new ones (scrollDepth, readComplete).
Article→article and article→conversion need an identity thread: a first-party visitor-ID cookie that stitches page views into a session and connects a read to a later conversion. Article→article (did reading A lead to clicking into B) is easy — internal link clicks within a session. Article→business conversion (did this read eventually lead to a signup/purchase) is the hard one, because for content/SEO the path is multi-touch and lagged — someone reads three articles over two weeks, then converts. Be honest about this: dwell and scroll are reliable and immediate; tying a read to a purchase weeks later is genuinely murky. So use engagement (read-through) as your near-term content-quality signal, and treat content→conversion as a softer, last-touch-attributed signal — clean conversion lives on email/paid, not organic content.
Same for posts, email, etc. — yes for the framework, no for equal fidelity
The pipeline is the same everywhere (events → identity → attribution → pattern scores), but how much you can actually see varies hard by how much of the channel you control:
Email is your cleanest conversion channel. Click tracking (wrapped links) is reliable, and the click→landing-page→goal path is tight and identifiable because you know who you sent to. One important current caveat: open-rate tracking is broken — Apple's Mail Privacy Protection pre-fetches images and inflates opens, so optimize email on clicks and conversions, never opens. You can't track "how long they read the email" either; you track clicks and what happened after.
Organic social is the weakest. You get platform-native metrics (impressions, likes, shares, comments, sometimes link clicks) via the platform APIs — but no dwell time (the platform doesn't expose "how long they read your post") and no reliable per-user conversion (you don't control distribution or get identity). Your only conversion path is UTM-tagged link clicks → your site, and it's lossy. Set expectations: organic gives you engagement signals, not read-time, and weak attribution.
Paid social/ads is clean on conversion — the pixel/CAPI reports per-user conversions back because you're paying and the platform closes the loop. Best attribution of the social surfaces.
Video/podcast give you watch-time/retention/completion from YouTube/Spotify analytics, but platform-mediated, and conversion only through description links + UTM.
So: full read-fidelity on surfaces you own (your site, email landing pages), click-and-conversion on email/paid, platform-metrics-only on organic. The unifying layer is one events pipeline plus a visitor-identity layer plus UTM attribution — each channel feeds what it can.
Scaling to 1000 concurrent — it's three problems, not one
The thing most people miss: 1000 concurrent users is not 1000 concurrent generations. At any instant the vast majority are reading, editing, or looking at dashboards — only a handful are actually triggering an LLM call. Size for that reality. There are three separate scaling axes:
1. Dashboard/API traffic — easy. Stateless HTTP scales horizontally: run multiple Next.js instances behind a load balancer, autoscale on load. 1000 people clicking around is trivial for any modern stack. The one real requirement is a connection pooler (PgBouncer or your platform's pooler) in front of Postgres — 1000 users opening naive per-request DB connections will exhaust Postgres long before CPU does. And cache the dashboard data (leaderboards, monitor snapshots) with a short TTL; don't recompute the snapshot for every one of 1000 dashboard loads.
2. LLM generation — the obvious bottleneck, and you buy your way out of it, not engineer. Generation is slow (10–30s) and rate-limited by the providers, not your servers. You already have the right answer: everything async through the job queue (pg-boss). User submits → job queued → worker processes at bounded concurrency → user notified via SSE/polling. With 1000 jobs queued, you process N-at-a-time (the ~15-concurrent ceiling per provider key). To raise throughput you (a) buy higher rate tiers, (b) split load across providers and keys (Gemini + OpenAI, multiple keys), and (c) — critically — add per-tenant fair queuing so one user's 500-article batch can't starve the other 999. You cannot make the models faster; you raise the ceiling and queue fairly underneath it.
3. Event ingestion — the sleeper problem that blindsides people. If your generated content is live on the web and getting traffic, the pageview/scroll/heartbeat beacons can be orders of magnitude more volume than your generation traffic — potentially millions of events a day from content you published months ago. This needs its own pipeline: a cheap ingestion endpoint that just accepts and enqueues (never processes inline), batched writes (not one DB insert per beacon), and ideally a separate datastore for high-volume events (a time-series store, ClickHouse, or at minimum heavily-batched Postgres on a different instance) so millions of raw beacons never touch your transactional primary. Miss this and your analytics traffic takes down your app DB.
Plus a quiet fourth: your learning/scoring jobs (mineCorpus, conversion labeling, monitor aggregation) are batch/scheduled — run them on their own queue and priority, off-peak, so they never compete with user-facing generation. Read replicas for the analytics queries help here too.
So the scaled architecture: stateless web tier behind a load balancer, PgBouncer in front of Postgres, all generation async through the queue with fair per-tenant scheduling and multi-provider capacity, a separate buffered pipeline for high-volume content events, read replicas plus caching for dashboards, and scheduled learning jobs isolated on their own priority. The mental model to hold: dashboard load scales horizontally (easy), generation scales by buying provider capacity + fair queuing (bounded), and event ingestion scales by a separate batched pipeline (the one people forget).

Engagement tells you what happened; feedback tells you why, in the user's own words. Let me lay out how it plugs in, where it's strong, and one real trap.
Where feedback fits in the signal stack
You'd now have three signal types, and they're complementary, not redundant:

Deterministic + judge (built) — catches defects automatically, cheap, but it's the system grading itself.
Engagement/conversion (Milestone B) — real behavior, but a proxy and lagged.
Human feedback (this) — direct quality judgment, immediate, but sparse (most people won't bother).

Feedback's superpower is that it's the only signal that's both direct and available before maturity — a thumbs-down arrives the moment someone reads, not weeks later when conversions trickle in. So it's especially valuable for new patterns that haven't accumulated engagement data yet, which is exactly where your cold-start problem lives.
Two distinct feedback sources — don't conflate them
Internal feedback (your platform users rating their own generated content). When ApexContent generates an article/post/podcast for a user, let them rate it — single piece or batch-review a queue. This is high-value because the user is a domain expert judging their own brand's content, and it's the most natural loop: they're already in the editor.
End-audience feedback (readers of the published content). A thumbs-up/down or "was this helpful?" widget embedded in published articles. Lower quality per-response (anonymous, noisier) but potentially higher volume, and it's real audience signal rather than the creator's opinion.
These feed the same learning center but mean different things — your user saying "this is off-brand" is a different lesson than 200 readers saying "this didn't answer my question." Tag them distinctly.
Batch and single — both, and batch is where the value concentrates
Single is the inline case: rate this piece right after generating it. Good UX, but you'll get sparse responses.
Batch is the more powerful pattern for your tool specifically. Since you generate content at scale, give users a review queue — "here are the 40 articles generated this week, swipe through and rate." This is how you get volume of high-quality labels, and it doubles as the human-in-the-loop quality gate before publishing. The batch reviewer is genuinely a feature, not just data collection: it's where a user approves/rejects/flags at scale, and every one of those actions is a training label.
For both, capture more than a star rating where you can. A thumbs-down with a reason ("too generic," "wrong tone," "factually off," "weak hook") is worth far more than a bare 1-star, because it maps directly onto your dimensions. "Too generic" → humanness. "Factually off" → factuality. "Weak hook" → engagement. That reason code is what lets feedback update the right dimension's Wilson score instead of a vague global one.
How it loops back — it reuses everything you've built
The beautiful part: feedback doesn't need a new learning system. It feeds the exact same patternDimensionStats attribution path as the judge and the engagement labeler:

A piece gets feedback → translate it to per-dimension outcomes (the reason codes map to dimensions; a bare rating maps to an overall pass/fail).
Pull that piece's patternsUsedJson (the patterns the agent injected).
Run the same attributeReview → Wilson update you already wrote — feedback is just another source of success/fail per dimension.
Strongly negative feedback → also write to the Guardian defect ledger, so it becomes an [AVOID] constraint on the next generation. ("Users keep flagging 'too salesy'" → inject "avoid salesy CTAs.")
Exceptionally-rated pieces → capture as exemplars (the few-shot winners), so the next generation imitates what humans loved.

So the answer to "make the next articles come out better" is concrete: feedback updates which patterns rank, adds avoid-constraints for what people disliked, and promotes the loved pieces into the prompt as examples. All three are mechanisms you already have — feedback just becomes a fourth writer into them.
The weighting question — feedback should count more per response
A single thumbs-down from your actual user is a stronger signal than one ambiguous engagement data point, so it should carry more weight. Two honest ways to handle it:

Source-weighted updates — a human rating counts as several "trials" in the Wilson math rather than one, reflecting that direct judgment is more informative than a single proxy observation. (Keep it bounded, or one angry user skews everything.)
Override on conflict — when human feedback and the judge disagree, trust the human. Your user saying "this is bad" beats GPT saying "this is a 78." Use feedback to recalibrate the judge — if humans consistently rate higher/lower than the judge scores, your judge rubric is miscalibrated, and that's a signal worth acting on.

The trap: feedback is sparse, biased, and gameable
This is the part to be honest about, because naive feedback loops degrade fast:
Response bias. The people who bother to rate are disproportionately the annoyed ones (and occasionally the delighted). Silence isn't a "neutral 3" — it's missing data. Don't treat un-rated content as failure, and don't let the vocal minority dominate; a piece with 3 ratings shouldn't move a pattern's score as much as one with 50. (Wilson already handles this — low sample stays humble — which is exactly why you built it.)
Volume is low. Most generated pieces will get zero feedback. So feedback supplements engagement/judge signals on the pieces that have it; it can't be the sole signal or most patterns never get scored. Use it as a high-weight input where present, not the backbone.
It's gameable / noisy at the audience level. Public thumbs widgets attract spam and brigading. For end-audience feedback, rate-limit, require some friction, and weight it below your own users' ratings.
Don't optimize purely to ratings. Same Goodhart risk as everything else — if you chase the rating, you evolve content that feels good to skim-rate, not content that performs. Feedback is one input among three (judge, engagement, feedback), and the conversion signal stays the north star.

It flows through the same labeler → attributeReview → Wilson + ledger + exemplar path the judge and engagement use.
